Dorothy Vaughn
"THENASAMANAGER"

HER STORY

Dorothy Johnson Vaughan was born in Kansas City, Missouri, on September 20, 1910. She grew up in a racially segregated society but excelled in her studies, particularly in mathematics. She attended Wilberforce University in Ohio, graduating with a bachelor's degree in mathematics in 1929.

NASA

Dorothy Vaughan began her career at the National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics (NACA), which later became NASA, in 1943. She was initially hired as a mathematician and worked in the segregated West Area Computing Unit alongside Katherine Johnson and other African-American women.

Supervisor of the West Area Computing Unit

Dorothy Vaughan's leadership skills and mathematical expertise quickly became evident. In 1949, she was promoted to head of the West Area Computing Unit, making her the first African-American woman to supervise a group of staff members at NACA. In this role, she oversaw a team of African-American women who performed vital mathematical calculations for various projects, including early space missions.

Transition to Electronic Computing

With the advent of electronic computers in the 1950s and 1960s, Dorothy Vaughan recognized the importance of learning computer programming languages. She and her team transitioned from manual calculations to working with IBM computers, making her one of the early pioneers of computer programming at NASA.

Contributions to the Space Race

Vaughan's work in programming and her team's contributions were instrumental in the success of the Mercury and Apollo space programs. Her expertise and leadership played a crucial role in helping NASA stay competitive in the Space Race with the Soviet Union.

Recognition

While the contributions of Dorothy Vaughan and other “Hidden Figures” remained relatively unknown for many years, their stories gained widespread recognition and appreciation following the release of the 2016 movie "Hidden Figures". Their remarkable achievements have since been celebrated, and their legacies have inspired a new generation of women and minority students to pursue careers in STEM fields.

Impact & Legacy

Dorothy Vaughan retired from NASA in 1971 after a distinguished career spanned nearly three decades. Her contributions, particularly in computer programming and mathematics, were essential to the success of early space missions. Still, her legacy as a pioneering mathematician and computer programmer continues to inspire and serve as a symbol of excellence in the face of adversity.
