Katherine Johnson
"THENASASCIENTIST"

HER STORY

During racial segregation and discrimination, Katherine Coleman Goble Johnson was born on August 26, 1918, in White Sulphur Springs, West Virginia. From a young age, she displayed a remarkable aptitude for mathematics and a passion for learning. Her parents, Joshua and Joylette, encouraged her academic pursuits.

Despite her exceptional talent, Johnson faced challenges accessing quality education because of racial segregation. However, her family's determination and dedication led her to attend high school on the campus of West Virginia State College. She graduated summa cum laude with degrees in mathematics and French from West Virginia State College (now West Virginia State University) in 1937.

NASA

TRAILBLAZER

In 1953, Katherine G. Johnson began her career at the National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics (NACA), which later became NASA. She initially worked as a “human computer” in the segregated West Area Computing Unit, where Johnson's mathematical skills were used to calculate complex equations needed for aircraft design and engineering.

Katherine Johnson's contributions to NASA were groundbreaking. She calculated the trajectories for the early space missions of NASA, including the historic flight of astronaut Alan Shepard, the first American in space. Her precise calculations were essential for the success of these missions and laid the foundation for the future of human spaceflight.

Her most famous work was her involvement in the Apollo 11 mission to the Moon in 1969. Her calculations were instrumental in ensuring that the spacecraft successfully landed and returned safely to Earth.

RECOGNITION

Katherine Johnson's work and contributions were not widely recognized during her early career due to the segregation and discrimination of the time. However, in recent years, her achievements have received well-deserved recognition. In 2015, she was awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom by President Barack Obama. Additionally, she received numerous other honors and awards, including the Congressional Gold Medal in 2019.

Impact & Legacy

Katherine G. Johnson's legacy is a testament to her extraordinary talent and perseverance. She broke down racial and gender barriers in mathematics and made invaluable contributions to space exploration. Her story and those of her colleagues at NASA were celebrated in the 2016 movie “Hidden Figures,” which brought her life and achievements to a broader audience.
